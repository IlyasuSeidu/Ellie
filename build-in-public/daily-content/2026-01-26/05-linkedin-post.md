# LinkedIn Post — January 26, 2026

Most onboarding problems are not information problems.

They are weight problems.

I am building Ellie, a shift scheduling tool for mining workers. The onboarding has one job: get someone from zero to a working schedule before they lose confidence in the product.

The shift pattern step was the bottleneck.

The information was correct. A miner needed to pick their roster cycle so Ellie could build their schedule accurately. But the interaction asked them to study a dense selector and make a formal choice about something they have always known by feel.

4 on, 4 off. Days into nights into off days. The rhythm of the week.

Nobody thinks in pattern names first. They think in lived experience.

So I rebuilt that step into swipeable cards. Not for aesthetics. To match how the decision actually lives in someone's head. Recognize your schedule instead of decoding it.

The lesson that stayed with me:

A product can be technically correct and still feel like admin. And once it feels like admin, people start quietly asking whether finishing setup is worth it. That doubt does not announce itself. It just shows up as drop-off.

The fix is rarely more explanation. It is changing the weight of the interaction itself.

Built by a miner for shift workers. I know what it feels like to open something after a long shift and immediately want to close it again.
