# LinkedIn Ready Post

## Strategy

- Angle Mix: Founder Opinion + Mini Case Study
- Format: text post with one real product lesson
- Comment prompt: Where does your product still charge a cognitive tax in minute one?

## Primary Post

A product can be technically correct and still fail the first minute.

That was the issue in Ellie this week.

Our shift calculations were reliable, but early onboarding still asked people to carry too much context too quickly. For shift workers opening the app between real-life fatigue and time pressure, that hesitation is costly.

We ran a clarity pass instead of a feature pass:

- setup state now carries cleanly from one screen to the next
- roster-time inputs align better with how shifts are actually lived
- the answer screen reads faster because date and shift logic are easier to interpret
- welcome and pricing moments were tightened for faster comprehension
- language quality was completed across 11 locales

None of this changed the headline capability. It changed whether people trust the capability fast enough to continue.

The lesson: minute-one cognitive load is a product metric, not just a UX polish topic.

I build Ellie as a miner solving a problem I live every week. AI-native tooling helped the speed, but judgment came from user reality.

Where does your product still charge a cognitive tax in minute one?

## Builder Variation

This week in Ellie, I did less feature work and more comprehension work.

The shift engine was already correct. The weak point was first-run clarity.

I tightened setup carryover, roster-time inputs, and answer-screen readability so people can understand faster without extra explanation. Then I closed remaining language clarity gaps across 11 locales and refined welcome/pricing comprehension.

Same capability. Better trust velocity.

What I keep learning: if users need to stop and decode, we have pushed thinking cost onto them.

Where are you still doing that in your product?
